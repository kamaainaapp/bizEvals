Background & Purpose
Small businesses do not sit at the edge of the U.S. economy. They are the engine of it.
There are 36.2 million small businesses in the United States. They make up 99.9 percent
of all U.S. firms and employ 62.3 million people, roughly 46 percent of the private-sector
workforce.
1 They generate 43.5 percent of GDP and were responsible for 88.9 percent of
net new jobs created between March 2023 and March 2024.
2
And yet, AI is barely reaching them. Only 8.8 percent of small businesses used AI in the
production of goods or services as of August 2025, compared to about 18 percent of
firms at year-end 2025.
3 The deeper issue is not simply access. It is relevance. Nearly 82
percent of businesses with fewer than five employees said AI does not seem relevant to
what they do.
4 That is not a lack of imagination from small business owners. It is a
coverage gap in how AI is being trained, modeled, and positioned.
When small businesses finally do adopt AI, the payoff is real. More than 80 percent report
productivity gains, and 16 percent report gains of more than 20 percent.
5 The promise is
there. The question is whether the technology is being built for the people who need it
most.
The purpose of this work is to expand AI training beyond the narrow slice of the economy
32it currently reflects: enterprise software, knowledge workers, and the technology
sector. That slice represents roughly 20,000 large firms.
1 The other 36.2 million
businesses - the shops, trades, service providers, solo operators, family businesses, and
local employers that keep the country moving, are too often treated as peripheral, even
though they are anything but.
This project pushes AI training and modeling toward the domains that have been
overlooked: the trades, retail, construction, transportation, professional services, and the
small-scale operators who carry complex businesses with limited infrastructure. These
are not edge cases. They are the daily realities of American work.
AI cannot fulfill its productivity promise if it only works for the businesses already fluent in
technology. It has to work for the people fluent in craft, service, grit, and survival. This
means building AI for the plumber who has perfected the work, but struggles with the
business. For the mom-and-pop entrepreneur whose job title is ‘everything’. For the
contractor estimating jobs after working a 12 hour day, the retailer managing inventory
between customers, the solo consultant balancing service delivery with marketing,

one of the largest forces in the American economy.
If AI is going to make an impact beyond boardrooms and software companies, it must be

broader, more grounded future: one where AI is not just powerful, but useful; not just
intelligent, but relevant; not just built for scale, but built for the people who have been

Limits & Tradeoffs
Every reputable project knows where its limitations are and is upfront about how and
where they can affect results. The same factors that make our work so valuable are the
same factors that restrict us, and instead of pretending they don’t exist, we disclose
them. There are three limiting factors and tradeoffs that are important to discuss: the size
and reach of our team, the inherent subjectivity that comes with the territory we work in,
and the true economic value this work can bring.
Team Size and Domain Coverage
The sum total of every small business in the US is immense, and we are only 15 people.
That doesn’t even begin to put a dent in the coverage of small business procedures, and
we know it. Each of our experts brings deep knowledge of their own corner, but no group
this size can represent every trade, every industry, every region, and every way a small
business gets run. There will be gaps. There will be domains we barely touch and others
we don’t reach at all. The depth we bring in the areas we cover is real, but it comes at the
cost of breadth. Instead of wide and shallow coverage, we’re striving for highly robust,
difficult, narrow, and deep subject matter coverage. We’re not going to pretend we cover
everything. The point is to make the areas we do cover unmistakably realistic and
incredibly difficult.
Subjectivity
The second limit is built into the nature of the work itself. Unlike finance, math, medicine,
or science, we are not operating in fields where every question leads to a single,
unambiguous, and clear answer. We are translating lived and professional experience
into situational prompts, evaluation criteria, and training signals.
That is the purpose of our work, and it is also where the complexity of our evaluations
begins. Small business decisions are shaped by context: the customer, the market, the
timing, the risk, the relationship, the cash flow, the owner’s capacity, and the hard-earned
instincts that come from doing the work over and over again.
Because of that, two experts can look at the same prompt and reach different
conclusions, and both may be right. Two equally seasoned contractors may run the same
businesses totally differently and both be successful. A solo operator may make one
decision because time is scarce, while a larger-scale small business may make another
because staff capacity changes the equation.

This means we carry a higher rate of variation among expert opinion compared to other
fields anchored to objective math or standardized evidence. That is not a flaw in the
experts. It is the honest shape of the territory. The trade-off is that our data reflects
real-world judgment instead of a single enforced standard, which makes it more authentic
but harder to objectively evaluate.
The Limit of Measuring What Matters Most
We claim significant costs are saved and great economic value is created with our evals,
and we wholeheartedly believe in it. But we should be honest that the deepest value of
this work is the hardest to put a number on. We can show that the model beats another
model on our prompts. We can point to productivity gains in the research. The metric that
matters most is the hardest to cleanly estimate.
When a small business runs more efficiently, the owner gains something intangible that
doesn’t show up instantly on a balance sheet. Time. The operator who closes out his
invoices in minutes instead of hours makes it home for dinner. The mom and pop who
aren’t buried in paperwork at midnight get their evening back. The person who has
carried the whole business on their shoulders gets a little room to breathe, to be present,
to live. How can we put a price on that?
That is the real return, and we can’t fully quantify it. We see that not as a weakness in our
work, but as a reminder of why it infinitely matters. We are not just making a model better.
We are trying to hand time and peace of mind back to the people who keep this economy
standing.

Complexity & Richness
Small business isn’t one job. It’s a thousand jobs stacked on top of each other. The
same person who fixes the problem, also bills for it, markets it, hires for it, and worries
about making payroll on Friday. That is the kind of messy, layered, real-world complexity
that Grok needs to learn from, and it’s exactly the kind of messiness that other synthetic
training scenarios miss. Our work is built to capture it.
How We Do It
The technical process is straightforward, that’s the strength of it. We write single-turn
prompts for real tasks inside our domain, the actual problems a small business owner
faces in a given day. Then we grade the output and compare it against what other LLMs
produce. Every prompt comes from lived work, which means every evaluation is
grounded in something that actually happens, not something we imagined might happen.
That matters because the quality of an evaluation is only as good as the real-life
situations they are used for later. A model can look brilliant on a clean, academic test, and
then instantly fall apart the moment a real entrepreneur asks it a real question about a real
problem. We close that gap. By drawing prompts straight from the work, we surface the
places where Grok is genuinely strong and the places where it still needs to grow. That
comparison, done honestly against other models, is where the economic value and the
model improvement come from. We aren’t just measuring performance. We are pointing
at exactly where the next gain lives.
The Human Element
This is the most critical part, and it is also the reason the work matters. Small business is
as vast as our universe, touching every domain light can reach. Right now, we are 15
experts, basically a tiny beam of light, which only illuminates a fraction of the enormous
knowledge base that small business represents.
But that fraction is not random. It is 15 lived experiences. Fifteen former VPs, Directors,
MDs, and other experts bring years of judgment, pattern recognition, failure, adaptation,
and hard-earned professional instinct into the model-building process. Every prompt
carries the weight of real practice behind it: the kind of knowledge that comes from doing
the work, not just describing it.
That is something that other scraped dataset cannot replicate. A dataset is only as good
as the human expertise that gives it context. We know what matters, what is missing,
what the stakes are, and why a technically correct answer can still fail the person trying to
use it.

Where Can This Project Go?
Right now, Grok has made it to the moon. We have proven that this kind of
domain-grounded evaluation can be successfully built, and we have planted the xAI flag
in the lunar soil. But the real opportunity is much larger. We want to take this to Mars and
beyond.
The 15 of us are only the beginning, not the boundary. What we are building is a radically
new evaluation model for how human expertise can make LLMs better: more practical,
more grounded, more economically useful, and more responsive than other evals that
performs the work that actually keeps small businesses alive.
The future of AI will not be measured only by how well it serves the largest companies. It
will be measured by whether it can understand the people doing everything, everywhere,
all at once.

Diversity & Versatility
The incredible opportunity and value of this eval set is its range. Most evaluations stay
narrow. Finance tests finance, accounting tests accounting, with the primary focus on
enterprise use. The Business domain is incredibly vast, and most of it has been
overlooked or gone untested. This set currently covers twelve sub-domains of business,
targeting deliverables, real-life tasks, and time horizons from hours to two weeks. The
tasks evaluate the commonly overlooked parts of the economy: the trades, retail, service
providers, and solo operators, not just big corporate applications.

What Operators Actually Do
These twelve domains cover a wide array of jobs from an enormous range of businesses.
A solo consultant runs marketing, billing, and operations in the same week. A contractor
quotes jobs, manages a project, and chases payments. The owner of a business? Their
title might as well be "everything," because the job demands all of it, from executing a
vision to keeping the lights on, and building something from soup to nuts. Most AI training
and evals have yet to focus on this sector. They concentrate on STEM, coding, finance,
accounting, and enterprise applications, the areas where models have already made
exponential improvements on. This is the opportunity that Business Evals is currently
targeting.

Measured against Apex
Apex, from Mercor, is the strongest benchmark for top-tier professional work, but it
covers only four jobs: investment banking associate, management consultant, big law
associate, and primary care physician. Each case is written by an expert from firms like
Goldman Sachs, McKinsey, and Latham & Watkins to capture their day-to-day work. It
comes in two main forms. The core Apex is single-turn, with source documents and an AI
judge, where the best models currently score between about 65% and 77%.
Apex-Agents is the long-horizon version, set in a simulated world and covering the first
three of those jobs, where the strongest frontier models fall to around 50% at best, with
most landing in the mid-30s.
Apex goes deep on four elite professions at the very top of the labor market. Our
evaluation goes the other way. Only one of its four jobs, management consultant, falls
inside our twelve domains, and even there, Apex revolves around McKinsey and BCG
work, not the broader business economy our tasks are built on. Apex asks whether a
model can stand in for a Goldman analyst or a McKinsey associate. We ask whether it
can do the work of the businesses that make up most of the economy. Both questions
matter, but only the narrow one at the top of the market is being measured.

execute workflows, and Business Evaluations asks whether a deliverable reflects real
professional judgement.

Quality & Rigor
An evaluation is only as reliable as the tasks inside it and the grading behind them. A
weak prompt or faulty rubric distorts and deteriorates the result, and a score no one can
trust is worse than no score at all. For that reason, no task in this evaluation rests on a
single person's judgment. Every task is authored and reviewed by people who have done
the work, and it passes multiple independent reviews before being marked as complete.
Independent Reviews
Each task starts with an author (L0) who has done that job or has experience in the field.
They write the prompt and create the reference documents, a rubric that captures the
essentials and crux of the task, and often a golden deliverable.
From there, the task goes through multiple independent reviews. L1 reviews it first, then
L2, with a third reviewer adding a final check. Each reviewer checks the task for
correctness, real-world relevance, and difficulty, confirms it can be solved with the
materials provided, and makes sure the rubric accurately captures the key components
of what makes a deliverable great. A task is marked done once L1 and L2 have signed off.
Send-backs are part of the process: when a review turns up problems, the task returns to
the original author (L0) to be revised against the reviewers' feedback.
Multiple reviews ensure that human error is mitigated and alignment stays intact. A single
reviewer can miss things, and a single approval carries one person's blind spots or bias.
Independent experts working separately catch what any one of them would let through,
and their agreement helps maintain high quality across our data sets.
Meet the Expert Research Team Behind the Tasks
The reviewers are practitioners, not generalists. The team is composed of experts who
have spent real time doing the same kind of work, so the bar for correctness is set by
experience. The judgment comes from people who would be highly trusted to do the task
in a real-world scenario.

Grading & Scoring Integrity
Trust in the final scores comes from two places: the people who do the reviewing, and a
process where every task must clear multiple blind, independent reviews before it is
marked complete. The people bring the domain judgment. The reviews make sure no
single error or blind spot carries through. The result is a score that reflects real capability
on real work, which is the only kind worth handing to a decision.
